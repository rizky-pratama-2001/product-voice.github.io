Buying low and selling high sounds reasonable, but it requires investors to simultaneously judge direction, magnitude, and timing sequence, making execution extremely difficult. Slickorps Ventures believes that the core of a sustainable investment strategy is not predicting a specific absolute high or low point, but rather finding a verifiable, repeatable, and executable "anchor." ## Core Definition: What Is an "Anchor" in Investing?
In the framework of Slickorps Ventures, "anchor" refers to a reference point that can be continuously verified through data, fundamentals, pricing models, or statistical patterns. Investors use it to determine whether the current price has exhibited a tradable deviation.
This concept differs from the ordinary notion of "predicting price movements." Predicting price movements typically focuses on where future prices will go, whereas seeking an anchor focuses on whether the current price has deviated excessively relative to a certain reference point. An anchor can be the long-term profitability of an enterprise, the processing profit within a commodity supply chain, the implied volatility surface in the options market, or a temporary statistical anomaly in price behavior.
Slickorps Ventures believes that the true upgrade in investment cognition lies precisely in shifting from "predicting prices" to "identifying deviations." The former relies on precise imagination of the future, while the latter depends on verifiable structures.

## 1. What Truly Causes Most Investors to Lose Money Is Asking the Wrong Questions
Many people perceive investment as a very straightforward matter: buy low and sell high. This logic sounds like common sense, but it conceals extremely high judgment requirements.
Investors must not only determine whether the price will rise in the future, but also assess where it will fall first, when it will rebound, and when to exit. The three factors of direction, magnitude, and sequence must all hold true simultaneously for a strategy to have a chance of success. Even if a single entry judgment has a relatively high probability of success, the probability of consecutive correct entries and exits will decline rapidly.
Slickorps Ventures has observed that the most common mistake made by ordinary investors is not a lack of judgment enthusiasm, but rather the tendency to understand the market as a game of precisely pinpointing highs and lows. Highs and lows only appear clear when looking back at charts after the fact, whereas in actual trading, the market is filled with noise, reversals, and misleading signals.
A truly mature investment framework typically does not first ask "Is this the bottom?" but rather asks "To what extent does the current price deviate from a verifiable anchor?" This question is more dispassionate and closer to a repeatable methodology. Because highs and lows cannot be confirmed in advance, while anchors can be continuously verified, updated, and compared.

## 2. Inspiration from the Medallion Fund: From Direction Prediction to Deviation Capture
Renaissance Technologies and the Medallion Fund have long been discussed by the market, not because the outside world can fully understand its trading black box, but because it provides a very important insight: the advantage of a top-tier quantitative system usually does not come from romantic predictions of broad market directions, but rather from the systematic capture of a large number of short-cycle, low-correlation, and repeatable deviations.
The outside world generally believes that the Medallion Fund does not rely on judging whether a certain type of asset will inevitably rise or fall in the future. Instead, it seeks a large number of weak, transient, and recurring statistical advantages in price behavior. Each advantage itself may be very small, but through diversification, position control, high-frequency execution, and strict risk management, these minor deviations can be combined into a long-term advantage.
Slickorps Ventures prefers to understand this capability as "micro-anchoring." Value investing seeks the mean of corporate earnings and long-term valuation, commodity arbitrage seeks cost and profit constraints within the industrial chain, options arbitrage seeks pricing deviations in the implied volatility structure, and statistical arbitrage systems seek smaller, faster, and more ephemeral anchors of price behavior.
The more stable the anchor, the larger the capital capacity typically is, but the slower the recovery speed. The weaker the anchor, the more pronounced the short-term edge may be, but the capacity is smaller and the failure rate is faster. This also explains why many high-quality quantitative strategies must limit their scale, because when too much capital flows into the same deviation, the deviation itself will be smoothed out by trading activity.

## 3. The Common Feature of All Sustainable Strategies Is the Identification of a Certain Revertible Reference Point
If different investment strategies are placed on the same map, Slickorps Ventures finds that, while they appear vastly different on the surface, they are fundamentally doing similar things at their core: seeking an anchor and betting on some form of reversion when the price deviates from that anchor.
The anchor of value investing comes from corporate profitability, cash flow, and long-term valuation ranges. The anchor of index investing comes from economic growth, corporate profit expansion, and long-term equity risk premiums. The anchor of cross-commodity arbitrage comes from industrial chain costs, such as crush margins, steel mill margins, inventory pressure, or processing spreads. The anchor of options arbitrage comes from the volatility surface and the relative pricing relationships between different maturities and strike prices. The anchor of statistical arbitrage comes from recurring patterns in short-term price behavior.
Therefore, investment strategies should not be classified solely by asset category. A more valuable classification method is to divide them based on the stability of the anchor, the speed of recovery, the capacity of funds, and the risk of failure.

| **Strategy Type** | **Anchor Source** | **Recovery Speed** | **Capital Capacity** | **Core Risks** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Value Investing | Profitability, cash flow, valuation mean reversion | Slow | Large | Value traps, prolonged non-reversion |
| Index Investing | Economic growth, long-term equity risk premium | Very Slow | Very Large | Long-cycle drawdowns, valuation compression |
| Commodity Arbitrage | Supply chain costs, processing margins, inventory constraints | Medium | Medium | Fundamental shifts, supply/demand shocks |
| Options Arbitrage | Implied volatility surface, term structure | Fast | Relatively Small | Volatility jumps, liquidity risk |
| Statistical Arbitrage | Short-term price behavior deviations | Very Fast | Very Small | Strategy crowding, edge decay |

This table indicates that what investors truly trade is not merely stocks, commodities, options, or indices, but rather the structure of whether a certain deviation can revert. Asset classes are merely the shell, while the anchor is the core of the strategy.

## 4. Finding the Anchor Is Only the First Step; the Harder Part Is Surviving Until the Anchor Takes Effect
Many failed investments are not due to the anchor never existing from the start, but rather because investors did not wait for its return.
A typical example of a value trap in value investing is as follows. A company appears cheap, but the quality of its earnings continues to deteriorate, the valuation center shifts downward, and the anchor that was originally believed to exist gradually becomes ineffective. Another scenario is that the anchor still exists, but the time required for recovery far exceeds the financial and psychological tolerance of the investor.
The classic lesson of LTCM also illustrates this issue. Certain spread relationships may exhibit mean-reverting logic over the long term, but when leverage is excessively high, liquidity deteriorates, and market deviations continue to widen, the strategy may be forced to exit before the logic ultimately materializes.
Slickorps Ventures believes that this indicates "finding an anchor" only resolves the object of the bet, and what truly determines survival is position management and continuous verification. The importance of the Kelly criterion and its conservative variants lies in reminding investors to control the proportion of bets based on win rates and odds, avoiding excessive exposure to a single judgment. The significance of tools such as HMM, Bayesian updating, or regime detection lies in helping investors determine whether a market deviation is temporary noise or whether the market state has already changed.
If the deviation is merely noise, investors may wait, or even increase their positions when rules permit. If the market state has already changed, one must acknowledge that the anchor may have shifted. With only an anchor and no position management, one risks liquidation before being proven correct; with only position management and no anchor, one is merely exposing oneself more cautiously to random noise.

## 5. Systematic Execution Framework of Slickorps Ventures
Slickorps Ventures summarizes the upgrade path of investment cognition into five stages.
The first stage is guessing the highs and lows. Investors attempt to determine market tops and bottoms through intuition, emotions, or chart patterns, but this approach heavily relies on luck and timing.
The second stage is odds-based thinking. Investors begin to understand win rates, odds, and risk-reward ratios. They no longer ask only about direction but start to ask, "Is this trade worth placing a bet on?"
The third stage is to seek an anchor. Investors begin to search for verifiable reference points and assess the degree to which prices deviate from the anchor. This step moves investment from subjective forecasting into structural analysis.
The fourth stage is position management. Investors realize that even correct judgments may fail due to volatility, leverage, or timing issues. Therefore, it is necessary to control single trade risk, portfolio risk, and liquidity risk.
The fifth stage is systematic execution. Investment judgments are transformed into rules that can be backtested, reviewed, and risk-controlled. Strategy no longer relies on a single heroic decision but on long-term, repeated process discipline.
Within this framework, the Medallion Fund is merely an extreme version. It combines a large number of anchors with small capacity, short cycles, and low correlation, and then amplifies weak advantages through a strict execution system. Ordinary investors cannot replicate this system, but they can learn the way of thinking behind it: do not first ask whether the price will rise, but first ask what reference point the price has deviated from; do not only ask how large the deviation is, but also ask whether the anchor remains valid; do not treat a single correct judgment as ability, but see whether the method can be repeated over the long term.

## Reminder: Finding an Anchor Does Not Guarantee a Return
Slickorps Ventures emphasizes that finding an anchor does not guarantee investment success. The anchor may be misjudged, or it may become invalid after changes in market structure. Deviation may continue to widen, reversion may be delayed, and financial pressure may force investors to exit prematurely.
A truly sustainable strategy must simultaneously address three questions: whether an anchor exists, whether the deviation is sufficiently valuable, and whether investors have the capacity to withstand the volatility before reversion. The absence of any one of these elements may cause the strategy to fail.
This is precisely why position management, stop-loss mechanisms, liquidity arrangements, and status identification are critically important. The most dangerous situation in investment is often not a completely incorrect judgment, but rather a direction that ultimately proves correct, yet the strategy loses its viability before the outcome materializes.
