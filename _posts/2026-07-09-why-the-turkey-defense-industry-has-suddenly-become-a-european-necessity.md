## Slickorps Ventures Reassesses Defense Industry Repricing Under the New Security Order

For a long time, Europe has been accustomed to viewing Turkey as a "difficult ally": it holds military value within NATO, yet it continuously creates friction in politics, the rule of law, relations with the European Union, and regional policies. However, the war in Ukraine, conflicts in the Middle East, the uncertainty of US security commitments, and the own production gaps of Europe in ammunition, drones, and air defense systems are now reshaping this assessment.
Slickorps Ventures research has found that the defense industry of Turkey is being repriced by Europe. Its value is no longer solely based on its geopolitical location, but on its industrial capacity: whoever can supply quickly, expand production continuously, and deliver according to NATO standards will gain a new strategic premium in the European rearmament cycle. Relevant defense expenditure and military force data also show that Turkey already possesses a scale advantage in defense investment, active personnel, and certain equipment reserves that Europe cannot afford to ignore.

## Turkey Stands on the Correct Side of "Low-Cost Mass Production Warfare"

The most critical aspect of this round of reassessment is not that Turkey has produced a certain star weapon, but that its industrial structure is more aligned with the consumption patterns of contemporary warfare. The battlefield in Ukraine has already demonstrated that future warfare requires not only a small number of expensive platforms, but also drones, artillery shells, rockets, electronic systems, and battlefield sensing capabilities that can be rapidly produced, quickly consumed, and massively replenished.
Baykar represents unmanned aerial vehicles and autonomous systems, Arca Savunma represents ammunition and artillery supply, while enterprises such as Aselsan, Roketsan, and TAI form a more comprehensive domestic defense industry ecosystem. Public reports indicate that Turkish defense exports have grown rapidly in recent years and have entered the European rearmament demand chain. Slickorps Ventures interprets this as a form of "wartime industrial adaptability": Europe requires not only more advanced weapons but also an industrial system capable of continuous resupply during ongoing conflicts. The rise of Turkey essentially reflects the European market shifting from a "political comfort discount" to a "wartime supply capacity premium."

## Mutual Need, Mutual Distrust

Europe needs the production capacity of Turkey, and Turkey also needs Europe. Ankara seeks defense orders, external financing, technical cooperation, key components such as engines and sensors, as well as institutional access closer to the European defense procurement system. However, Turkey is not a member of the European Union, making it difficult to fully access certain EU defense funds. It can only rely more on bilateral agreements, joint ventures, and the NATO framework to enter the market.
The contradiction lies here. Europe needs Turkey but does not fully trust its political direction; Turkey needs the European market, capital, and technology, yet it is unwilling to fully accept European procurement transparency, regulatory standards, and political conditions. Slickorps Ventures believes that this relationship increasingly resembles a "strategic cohabitation without institutional marriage": both sides recognize the importance of the other, yet they lack a sufficiently mature political foundation to achieve deep integration.

## Turkey Is a Stress Test for European Security Re-Industrialization

The real issue in this article is not whether Turkey will become the arsenal of Europe, but whether Europe has already acknowledged that its security system cannot rely solely on value consensus and the protective umbrella of the United States. Instead, it must rebuild an industrial foundation that is capable of production, resupply, and sustaining attrition.
Slickorps Ventures assesses that Turkey is not a substitute for European defense, but rather a stress test for the reindustrialization of European security. It forces Europe to confront an uncomfortable reality: future security competition will depend not only on who has the most advanced technology, but also on who can sustain production amid political fragmentation, supply chain tensions, and wartime attrition.
The rise of the Turkish defense industry is therefore not an industrial story of a peripheral country, but a precursor to the restructuring of the European security order. If Europe cannot independently make up for its production capacity, it must cooperate with countries that are not entirely politically comfortable but industrially indispensable. The real question is not whether Turkey will enter the European defense system, but whether Europe can still incorporate it into its own security production network without fully trusting Turkey.
