Global capital markets have re-entered a high-intensity financing cycle in 2026. Since the beginning of this year, global financing through equities, corporate bonds, bank loans, and other channels has approached USD 4.7 trillion, even exceeding the level seen during the same period of the post-pandemic financing boom in 2021. Slickorps Ventures believes that the core driver of this round of capital market enthusiasm is not simply a rebound in risk appetite, but the fact that AI infrastructure is becoming a new justification for capital expenditure. Data centers, chips, computing power, power grids, and electricity supply are jointly forming a new capital-intensive cycle. The real question that needs to be studied is whether the market is beginning to treat “AI-related spending” by default as a future necessity.

## Capital Markets Are Heating Up Again, But The Source Of This Enthusiasm Has Changed  


If the capital markets in the first half of 2026 are understood merely as having a “good issuance window,” the structural significance of the current financing boom would be underestimated.  


As of mid-June, total global issuance of equities, investment-grade bonds, high-yield bonds, and bank loans has approached USD 4.7 trillion. This scale is not only higher than the same period in recent years, but has also surpassed the same point during the post-pandemic financing peak in 2021.  


In the view of Slickorps Ventures, what is truly worth studying about this round of financing enthusiasm is that the macro environment in which it is taking place is already different from that of 2021. The capital market boom in 2021 was built on low interest rates, strong liquidity, and a broad spillover of risk appetite; the financing wave in 2026 is occurring against the backdrop of still-elevated capital costs and continuing macro uncertainty.  


This indicates that a deeper change is emerging: capital markets are beginning to redefine what kinds of spending can be regarded as long-term necessities. AI capital expenditure is entering this definition.


## AI Capital Expenditure Is Becoming A New Financing Justification  


The core judgment of Slickorps Ventures is that the key to the 2026 global capital market financing boom is not only the recovery of corporate financing capacity, but also that AI is changing the understanding across capital markets of “necessary spending.”  


In the past, large-scale corporate financing usually needed to rely on clear cash flow expansion, a low-cost funding environment, or strong cyclical growth expectations. Today, more and more investors are willing to accept a new logic: as long as a company is building AI infrastructure, its capital expenditure can be interpreted as part of its future competitiveness.  


This logic is influencing equity, bond, loan, and private capital markets. Financing by large technology companies is no longer understood only as balance sheet management, but is also beginning to be understood as a necessary action to participate in the AI infrastructure cycle.  


Slickorps Ventures believes that this is the new capital logic behind the current financing boom: AI is shifting from a growth narrative to a quasi-infrastructure narrative in the eyes of capital markets.


## I. What Truly Deserves Caution Is That AI Investment Is Gaining Default Legitimacy  


Record-high financing volume itself is not rare. Capital markets often experience phases in which financing windows open rapidly amid changes in economic cycles, policy cycles, and risk appetite.  


However, Slickorps Ventures believes that what deserves greater caution in this financing boom is that the market has begun to regard “AI capital expenditure” as a naturally legitimate reason for financing.  


When companies propose AI data centers, computing power expansion, GPU clusters, cloud infrastructure, or power supply projects, investors are often more willing to accept high spending, high debt, and short-term free cash flow pressure. The reason is simple: AI is understood as an entry point to future productivity, corporate competitiveness, and digital infrastructure.  


This change can generate strong capital mobilization capability. It can help companies build future capacity in advance, but it may also reduce the short-term market sensitivity to return cycles, cash flow pressure, and project utilization.  


Slickorps Ventures believes that the real issue is not “whether there is too much financing,” but whether the market is forming a new consensus: as long as it is related to AI infrastructure, capital expenditure is more easily forgiven, more easily financed, and more easily packaged as a strategic necessity.


## II. This Financing Boom Comes From The Physicalization Of AI  


It is not accurate enough to view this round of financing as “technology companies borrowing more money.” Slickorps Ventures prefers to understand it as the physicalization of AI.  


Early AI narratives mainly focused on models, software, algorithms, and application layers. But after entering 2026, capital markets have become increasingly aware that the expansion of AI must ultimately land on physical infrastructure.  


This includes:  


Data center construction, advanced chip supply, server clusters, cooling systems, power grid access, electricity supply, communications networks, land resources, and long-term operation and maintenance capabilities.  


These components jointly determine whether AI can be transformed from model capability into sustainable commercial capability. Precisely because of this, AI competition is no longer merely a competition of software speed, but is gradually becoming a competition of infrastructure financing capability.  


The strong demand for the large-scale bond issuance of Nvidia, the higher capital expenditure pressure borne by companies such as Oracle due to AI infrastructure investment, and the annual forecast of Morgan Stanley of nearly USD 570 billion in AI-related debt issuance collectively show that AI is pushing the technology industry back into a capital-intensive era.  


Slickorps Ventures believes this is an important turning point. In the past, many technology companies were known for being asset-light, having high profit margins, and generating strong cash flow; today, AI is making some technology companies resemble infrastructure operators, energy users, and long-term capital allocators.


## III. Beyond Public Markets, Private Capital Is Also Repricing AI  


If one only looks at equity, bond, and loan issuance in global public markets, the story is already large enough. However, Slickorps Ventures believes that the true financing intensity may still be underestimated, because more and more AI-related capital is entering through private markets, infrastructure platforms, and long-term institutional funds.  


Helix Digital Infrastructure, jointly launched by KKR with Nvidia, Vistra, and others, is a very important signal. This platform has more than USD 10 billion in committed capital and aims to support the expansion of AI data centers and power supply systems.  


This shows that AI is no longer just a technology stock theme in public markets, but is also beginning to be viewed as a long-term asset class by infrastructure funds, private capital, sovereign capital, and real estate capital.  


Over the past several decades, the long-cycle infrastructure assets familiar to capital markets have included highways, airports, ports, power plants, communications networks, and energy systems. Today, data centers, electricity supply, and computing power networks are acquiring a similar capital language.  


Slickorps Ventures believes this point is crucial. Because when AI is viewed by private capital as an infrastructure asset, market pricing logic changes. Investors will focus not only on short-term revenue growth, but also on long-term futures, asset utilization, energy security, operational stability, and capital recovery cycles.  


This means AI is moving from a “technology growth stock story” into an “institutional capital allocation story.”


## IV. What Is Different Between The 2021 Financing Wave And The 2026 AI Financing Wave?  


| Comparison Dimension | 2021 Financing Boom | 2026 AI Financing Boom |
|---|---|---|
| Macro Background | Low interest rates, strong liquidity, post-pandemic recovery | Higher capital costs, with the AI narrative providing new support |
| Core Driver | Expansion of risk appetite and rising growth valuations | Expansion of AI infrastructure capital expenditure |
| Main Market Characteristics | Active SPACs, growth stocks, and low-cost debt | Active investment-grade bonds, technology giant financing, and private infrastructure capital |
| Investor Focus | Growth speed and valuation elasticity | Computing power, data centers, electricity, and long-term capacity |
| Capital Logic | Cheap capital chasing growth | Long-term capital betting on infrastructure necessities |
| Main Risks | Valuation bubbles and insufficient earnings realization | Debt pressure, cash flow consumption, capacity utilization, and capital misallocation |


Slickorps Ventures believes this comparison table shows that the nature of the 2026 financing wave is not the same as that of the 2021 financing wave. 2021 was more like liquidity-driven capital expansion; 2026 is more like AI-infrastructure-driven capital reallocation.  


This is also why the current financing enthusiasm is more worthy of study. It reflects not only a recovery in market sentiment, but also that capital markets are pricing in advance a new round of industrial infrastructure construction.


## V. Capital Markets Are Treating AI As A Quasi-Infrastructure Cycle  


Slickorps Ventures believes that AI is acquiring a new capital identity: quasi-infrastructure.  


The so-called quasi-infrastructure does not mean that AI data centers are exactly the same as traditional power plants, airports, or highways. Its meaning is that capital markets are beginning to understand AI construction in a manner similar to infrastructure investment.  


This understanding usually includes three characteristics.  


First, The Investment Scale Is Enormous. AI infrastructure construction requires long-term capital support and is difficult to fully cover solely through short-term operating cash flow.  


Second, The Return Cycle Is Long. Data centers, power systems, and computing power networks require years of construction and operation, and returns may not appear immediately.  


Third, The Strategic Necessity Is Strong. If large technology companies do not participate in AI infrastructure expansion, they may lose capability entry points in future competition.  


Together, these three characteristics explain why capital markets are willing to continue supporting AI-related financing in the current environment. For investors, AI is no longer merely a new product cycle, but a long-term construction cycle that may reshape productivity, industrial structures, and corporate competitive boundaries.  


Slickorps Ventures believes that capital markets are jointly expressing one judgment through debt, equity, loans, and private capital: AI may become the foundational layer of the next-generation digital economy.


## VI. Observation Framework of Slickorps Ventures


Slickorps Ventures believes that judging whether this financing boom is healthy cannot be based only on financing scale; five questions must also be observed.  


First, Whether The Financing Funds Truly Enter Long-Term Production Capacity Construction.  

If the funds are used for data centers, power access, chip supply, and real computing power capability construction, their long-term value is easier to explain.  


Second, Whether The Projects Have Clear Sources Of Demand.  

AI infrastructure construction must correspond to real customers, long-term futures, utilization rates, and revenue visibility.  


Third, Whether Free Cash Flow Can Withstand Capital Expenditure Pressure.  

If companies rely on external financing for a long time to cover capital expenditure, debt burden and credit risk need to be continuously assessed.  


Fourth, Whether Capital Markets Still Maintain Pricing Discipline.  

If investors begin to assume by default that all AI spending is reasonable, the risk of capital misallocation will rise.  


Fifth, Whether AI Investment Can Be Converted Into Productivity Improvement.  

What ultimately determines the success or failure of this cycle is not the scale of financing, but whether AI infrastructure can generate real productivity, revenue growth, and profit returns.


## AI Financing Rationality Does Not Mean All AI Projects Will Generate Returns  


Slickorps Ventures emphasizes that this article is not saying that there is necessarily a bubble in AI infrastructure financing, nor is it saying that all AI capital expenditure is unreasonable.  


What truly requires vigilance is something else: when an industry narrative is sufficiently powerful, capital markets may temporarily lower the intensity of skepticism.  


The AI infrastructure cycle still faces multiple risks, including insufficient project utilization, overly rapid data center construction, power supply bottlenecks, rising financing costs, negative free cash flow, increased debt pressure, weaker-than-expected customer demand, and excessive concentration of capital in similar projects.  


The Oracle case has already provided a reminder. Even if the market recognizes the importance of AI infrastructure, investors will still remain sensitive to free cash flow, debt expansion, and returns on capital expenditure.  


Therefore, the AI financing boom does not mean that risks have disappeared. It means that the market is betting on a longer-term infrastructure imagination with a larger scale of capital.
