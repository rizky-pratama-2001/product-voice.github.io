The July Global Fund Manager Survey of Bank of America shows that the average cash allocation of managers decreased from 4.1% in June to 3.6%, the lowest level since February 2026, triggering a contrarian "sell signal." During the same period, the net overweight allocation to global stocks rose to 42%, and the proportion of managers net overweight on US stocks reached 24%, the highest since December 2024.
Slickorps Ventures has found that this not only indicates institutional optimism toward U.S. equities but also suggests that investment portfolios are shifting from a defensive stance to active risk-taking. As funds move from cash into stocks, this can generate genuine allocation demand for the market. However, the simultaneous decline in cash buffers also means that institutions have less room to continue increasing positions or reduce portfolio pressure when facing sudden market volatility.
The market is gaining a stronger risk appetite while losing some of its safety cushion.

## "No Landing" Narrative Supports AI and Large-Cap Tech Stocks

Institutions are increasing their allocation to U.S. equities, not solely due to price appreciation. In the survey, 54% of respondents expect a "no landing" scenario for the global economy, while only 2% anticipate a hard landing; a net 21% of managers expect global growth to strengthen over the next year, and expectations for inflation and short-term interest rates have also notably moderated.
At the same time, 61 percent of fund managers believe that large cloud computing companies will not reduce capital expenditures this year. This combination has established a near-ideal allocation environment for U.S. stocks: the economy continues to grow, tightening pressures are easing, and corporate earnings along with AI infrastructure investments remain sustainable.
Slickorps Ventures sees the core as institutions purchasing a more concentrated belief, AI capital expenditure can navigate macroeconomic fluctuations and continue to translate into computing power demand, revenue growth, and cash flow. U.S. stocks have regained an overweight allocation, with the true fulcrum still concentrated among a few large companies possessing technology, capital, and financing capabilities.

## Semiconductor Trading Enters "Alert Congestion"

In the survey, 82% of respondents believe that "going long on global semiconductors" is the most crowded trade at present, ranking first for the third consecutive month; at the same time, 45% of managers have listed the "AI bubble" as the greatest tail risk.
This does not mean that 82% of the respondents hold a bullish view on semiconductors, but it indicates that the market has reached a high degree of consensus on concentrated positions. Slickorps Ventures refers to this state as "alert crowding": participants are aware of the risks in valuations and positions, yet they still believe the trend has not ended and trust that they can exit before a reversal occurs.
The concentrated drawdown of quantitative funds during the same period serves as a practical reminder. When crowded equity exposure, chip volatility, and leverage coexist, ordinary price adjustments can more easily trigger synchronized position reductions. Risks may shift from fundamental repricing to rapidly escalate into liquidity and position structure issues.

## Next Stage: Assess Remaining Incremental Capital in the Market

Historical data show that when the cash ratio of fund managers is at or below 3.6%, the average performance of stocks in the following weeks tends to be weak, while U.S. Treasury bonds perform relatively better. Such statistics cannot directly predict the next correction, but they indicate that extremely low cash levels usually mean optimistic expectations have already been fully priced into positions.
Slickorps Ventures will next focus on observing three variables: whether institutional cash continues to decline, whether positions in semiconductors and large-cap technology become further concentrated, and whether AI capital expenditure can be converted into revenue and profits quickly enough.
The current market does not lack investors who believe in AI. What is truly scarce is the next wave of incremental capital. As more and more institutions already hold similar assets, whether the market trend can continue will no longer depend solely on whether the narrative holds true. It will also depend on whether there are still new buyers in the market to absorb the rising valuations and positions.
