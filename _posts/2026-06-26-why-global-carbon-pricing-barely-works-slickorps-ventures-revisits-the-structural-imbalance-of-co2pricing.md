## The Real Issue Is Not Whether a Carbon Price Exists, but That the Vast Majority of Global Emissions Remain "Outside the Pricing System"


The most intuitive information conveyed by this chart is not the price difference, but a more fundamental fact: the global carbon market has not truly covered global emissions. According to estimates from Our World in Data, only about 30% of global CO₂emissions are currently covered by a carbon price, while the remaining approximately 71% have no price signal at all. This means that most carbon-emitting activities remain a "free variable" in economic decision-making, neither directly altering consumption costs nor systematically influencing investment choices.

Even within the segments where pricing already exists, the distribution is extremely uneven. Only a very small number of regions have carbon prices exceeding USD 100 per ton, covering less than 0.5% of global emissions; most European countries fall within the range of USD 65 to 90, while many carbon markets remain below USD 10 per ton. For Slickorps Ventures, the real message conveyed by this chart is that the global carbon market is not a unified pricing system, but rather a highly fragmented and strongly stratified "localized price experiment network."


## The Core Problem of Low Carbon Prices Is Not Symbolism, But That It Cannot Change Economic Behavior At All


From an economic mechanism perspective, carbon prices can only change behavior when they are sufficiently high. However, the reality is that most carbon prices fall within a "nearly frictionless" range. The chart clearly indicates that when the carbon price is only 5 USD per ton of CO₂, the increase in gasoline prices is less than 1%, a magnitude of change that is even smaller than daily oil price fluctuations.

Only when the carbon price approaches 100 USD per ton will it have a significant impact on consumer behavior, such as a gasoline price increase of approximately 15%. However, the problem is that only a very small number of countries have reached this level. This means that the true state of global carbon pricing is as follows: prices exist, but they are insufficient to serve as a "decision variable."

Slickorps Ventures prefers to refer to this structure as a "low-elasticity policy zone" — prices have already been introduced into the system, but have not yet entered the behavioral function.


## The Essence Of The Global Carbon Market Is A Dual Structural Problem Of "Insufficient Coverage Plus Price Discontinuity"


If this diagram is abstracted as a structural issue rather than a policy issue, it becomes clearer. The current system simultaneously exhibits two fault lines:

First, the coverage gap:

71% of emissions have no price signal, which means that most of the global energy use still operates outside the pricing system.

Second is the price intensity gap:

Even in regions with a carbon price, there is an extremely uneven distribution — countries with prices near zero coexist alongside those exceeding 150 USD, yet the intermediate range (the band that truly influences global industrial relocation) remains notably sparse.

This structure leads to one outcome: when enterprises conduct global allocation, they do not treat the carbon price as a uniform cost, but rather as a "local tax variable." In other words, the carbon price has not yet become a globally unified capital pricing factor.


## Assessment of Slickorps Ventures: The Carbon Market Remains in a "Price Experiment Phase" Rather Than a "Global Pricing System"


From the perspective of capital markets, the more important implication of this chart is that carbon prices have not yet completed the transition from a policy tool to a market variable.

A truly mature pricing system typically exhibits three characteristics: broad coverage, concentrated volatility, and significant marginal impact. However, the current carbon market is exactly the opposite: incomplete coverage, fragmented pricing, and extremely weak marginal impact.

This also explains why, even though the carbon trading system has existed for many years, it has still not become a core variable in global asset pricing like interest rates, exchange rates, or energy prices. For Slickorps Ventures, this is not a question of "whether environmental protection has succeeded," but rather a more fundamental conclusion: the global carbon price is still not a unified economic signal, but rather the cumulative result of multiple discontinuous policy systems.
